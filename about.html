About the Linwood Institute

Founded in 2013 by cognitive systems theorist Dr. Leo Linwood, the Linwood Institute is an independent research organization dedicated to advancing the science of memory, perception, and neural adaptation.

Operating at the intersection of neuroscience, behavioral modeling, and computational simulation, the Institute supports a wide range of internal and partner-based initiatives—ranging from early learning systems to complex identity architecture research. Many of Linwood’s internal protocols are developed in collaboration with ethics consultants, digital cognition specialists, and cross-institutional review boards.

In 2019, the Institute received a $12 million grant through an undisclosed federal-private partnership to begin development of the Recall Modulation Index (RMI) project. The initiative represents a groundbreaking approach to cognitive architecture—focused not on how memories are formed, but how they can be selectively isolated, delayed, or reintroduced under controlled conditions. The RMI study was approved as a five-year trial, with data collection and final system analysis scheduled to conclude on April 10, 2024.

From its inception, the Institute has maintained an intentionally low public profile, allowing researchers to focus on longitudinal studies without distraction. While several of Linwood’s core breakthroughs have quietly shaped therapeutic practices worldwide, much of the Institute’s ongoing work remains classified or pre-publication.

Dr. Linwood continues to serve as Director of Experimental Neurocognitive Systems, overseeing advanced platform development and supervising several long-range cognitive adaptation frameworks.

About the Recall Modulation Index (RMI)

The Recall Modulation Index (RMI) is a research project developed by the Linwood Institute to explore how memories can be temporarily hidden, isolated, or delayed—without being erased.

While most memory research focuses on how information is stored or retrieved, RMI takes a different approach: what happens if we simply make a memory… inaccessible? And what does the brain do in the meantime?

RMI is built on a concept we call memory vaulting—a process where specific memories are sealed away from active recall, but left intact beneath the surface. Over time, researchers have observed how behavior, identity, and perception shift when certain memories are quietly “vaulted.”

To study this effect, researchers also use carefully selected stimuli—sounds, images, and phrases—to bring those sealed memories back into conscious awareness at specific points in time.

If successful, RMI could be used to help:

  • Reduce the impact of traumatic experiences
  • Support learning and behavior change without distress
  • Create safer training environments for high-pressure jobs
  • Improve treatment for memory-based disorders

The Linwood Institute believes memory isn’t just something we keep—it’s something we can shape.
And perhaps one day, even you will be able to benefit from what we’ve learned.
